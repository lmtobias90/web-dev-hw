DIRECTIONS

Special Note: Sourdough takes a long time and multiple different steps before you actually end up with a loaf of bread. Unless your full-time job is as a baker, you'll need to play with the rhythm of each step to figure out how it fits into your schedule. Each step has the approximate time allotted in parenthesis {ex. (2 hours)} to give you a sense of about how long you should be at home and awake to complete each step. You can refrigerate your dough in between steps for up to 24-48 hours, until you have enough time to complete the next step in full. There are lots of different baking rhythms you can find to fit your schedule, but ultimately it's up to the baker!


  1. Refresh Your Sourdough Starter (4-12 hours)
  2. Mix Your Dough (1 hour)
    • Mix 250g All-Purpose flour + 250g Bread Flour + 400g warm water and knead until fully incorporated. Place a damp kitchen cloth over the bowl and let sit (or "autolyse") for at least 45 minutes.
    • Mix in 75g activated sourdough starter + 10g salt until the dough is smooth and springy.
    • Re-feed your remaining sourdough starter (Step 1) and leave on counter to rise if baking in the next 24 hours. Otherwise, store in the fridge after refreshing.
      Follow Step 1 to feed starter on a weekly basis or when baking your next loaf, whichever comes first.

  3. Stretch & Fold Dough (2 hours) |
    • Every 30 minutes for 2 hours, stretch & fold the dough to develop the strength of the gluten.
    • Cover with a damp cloth in between stretch & folds.

    Demonstration: Stretch & Fold Technique


  4. First Dough Rise
    • After final stretch & fold, cover bowl with a damp cloth (or a cover if storing in fridge).
    • Let sit no more than 4-6 hours on the counter, or 8-24 hours/overnight in the fridge

  5. Final Shaping (30 minutes)
    1. Transfer dough onto a clean, floured surface.
    2. Shape dough to fit baking container, gently stretching and folding under itself to create tension on the surface of the loaf.
    3. Let shaped dough sit on counter for 10-20 minutes
    4. Using dough scraper, transfer to a heavily floured banneton bowl or other final proofing bowl (lined with parchment paper or flour).
    5. Let proof at room temperature for 1-3 hours, or in the fridge for up to 24 hours.
      *Dough will proof much slower in the fridge, so don't proof in the fridge unless it has ample extra time to rise!
    6. Preheat oven to 450℉ ONE HOUR before you'll be baking your bread (with youur baking vessel inside). Bread likes a hot oven!

  6. Bake your loaf! (Finally!) (2 hours)
    1. Preheat your oven to 450℉, with the dutch oven inside.
    2. Drop your dough from the banneton (or proofing) bowl onto two crossed strips of parchment paper on the counter.
    3. Score your dough
    4. Using the strips of parchment, gently transfer your dough into your dutch oven or other baking vessel.
    5. Bake at 450℉ for 30 minutes with the top on.
    6. Remove the lid, turn down to 400℉, bake for 20 more minutes or until sufficiently browned
    7. Let the loaf cool completely before cutting into the loaf!


Other Notes:

  • Always work dough with slightly wet hands - it gets extremely sticky!
  • You can double the recipe for more loaves; and you can half the refreshed starter measurements to control how much sourdough discard you end up with, if you're not baking consistently.
  • This bread will go stale within a few days. If you won't eat it in time, slice it and freeze it, so you can pull out a slice to reheat as needed!
