The Fort Peck Assiniboine & Sioux Buffalo Nation

Data on community perceptions.

Authored by: The Fort Peck Pté Group

Published April 2021

For centuries, buffalo were highly regarded within the Tribes’ community, serving as keystone figures in ceremonies, traditions, and spirituality within the Tribes’ cultures. To members, the buffalo were also imperative for survival, serving as a significant source of clothing, food, and shelter. No part of the buffalo was ever wasted – the hide and bones were used to make shelters, clothing, everyday items, and for ceremonies, while the meat was used for nourishment [1]. The return of the buffalo to the Tribes’ lands after their extermination from those lands in the late 1800s offers members the opportunity to once again utilize these invaluable animals to support healthy minds, bodies, and spirits.

Traditionally and even today, buffalo are an important source of food and nutrition among community members [2]. Many of the Tribal Members understand the health benefits of consuming buffalo, as the meat is leaner and higher in protein compared to other meat sources such as beef. The arrival of buffalo back to the Tribes’ lands have many members wondering how to re-incorporate buffalo meat into the diets of the Tribal Community. Numerous feasibility studies, taste tests, and processing surveys have been conducted to answer this question. From these results, we can better identify how buffalo meat is currently being used, as well as determine if processing buffalo meat on the Tribes’ lands is an attainable goal for the future.

After gauging community attitudes towards buffalo using surveys and questionnaires since 2015, we know that the buffalo are still regarded as sacred among Tribal members, and that their meat is considered a healthy food that should be made easily available to the Tribe. Many people enjoy the taste, even if it means adding a little sauce or seasoning, or preparing it differently from beef to make it "just right".

Historically, buffalo have served as an important structure within the Tribes' communities. Not only did the buffalo provide food, clothing, and shelter, but they were also culturally valuable as teachers who offered lessons on humility and fortitude, while inspiring spirituality [1]. More than a century after their extermination, the buffalo were momentously returned to Fort Peck in the early 2000s and 2010s, and with them, a chance to finally reconnect with and reincorporate the buffalo back into the lives of Tribal Members [2].

An essential step in the right direction has been the regulated hunting of buffalo. Introduced in 2003 as a systemic way to contribute to the financial sustainability of the Tribes’ buffalo program, the fee-hunting system is an important source of revenue that simultaneously ensures the annual availability of buffalo for meat donations [3]. Participation in the Tribal Member Buffalo Hunts Lottery guarantees that each tribally enrolled member of the Tribes who applies has an equal chance of being selected for the year’s annual hunt. Since 2013, these hunts have been formally recorded to include the names of hunt applicants, winners, and alternates. From this data, we can better understand the reach of the lottery, and we can show how this portion of the buffalo program has grown and developed over time.
